Car Insurance for New Drivers

Car insurance is a legal necessity for all drivers, providing protection for other road users and pedestrians. New drivers face higher costs due to their limited experience, lacking a no-claims history. There are three insurance levels: third party only (TPO), third party, fire and theft (TPFT), and comprehensive. TPO covers others' injuries and damage, TPFT adds coverage for fire and theft, while comprehensive includes additional protections for personal injury and vehicle damage. New drivers can be added to parents' insurance alternatively, individual insurance helps build a personal no-claims history. Ensure each driver is only insured once to avoid claims issues. With accrued experience, insurance costs typically decrease with a growing no-claims bonus. .

Cockpit Safety Check

Before starting the engine, you should make a series of checks known as the cockpit drill. On your driving test, when you first enter the car, you must carry out the cockpit drill to show the examiner that you are familiar with the routine:

  • Doors
    Make sure the boot, the bonnet, and all doors are closed properly. Many cars will show a dashboard warning light if a door or the boot is open, so check your instrument panel once the engine is on.
  • Seat
    Adjust your seat so you can comfortably reach all the controls. You should be able to fully depress the clutch and accelerator pedals without having to stretch. Adjust your head restraint to be in the correct position.
  • Seat
    Adjust your seat so you can comfortably reach all the controls. You should be able to fully depress the clutch and accelerator pedals without having to stretch. Adjust your head restraint to be in the correct position.
  • Steering Wheel
    If the steering wheel is adjustable, adjust it so your hands are resting comfortably, a little lower than your shoulders. Make sure the wheel doesn't block any part of the instrument panel.
  • Seatbelt
    Put your seatbelt on, making sure the belt isn't twisted. The belt should be strapped firmly against your body. The law states that whilst driving a car, you must wear your seatbelt at all times, except when performing a reversing manoeuvre or if you have a medical exemption certificate. It is also your responsibility to ensure that any passenger under the age of 14 is wearing a seatbelt.
  • Mirrors
    Check your left, centre, and right mirrors are correctly aligned so you can quickly see what's happening behind you with a glance without having to move your head unnecessarily. Side mirrors / adjust them so you can see a small amount of side of the car is showing and that there is an equal proportion of road and sky visible. Interior centre mirror / adjust this so you can see the top edge of the rear window at the top edge of the mirror. Hold the mirror by the edges to avoid finger marks.
  • Handbrake
    Make sure the handbrake is engaged and that the gear stick is in neutral or park or neutral if driving an automatic. If the car is in gear when you start the engine, the car will lunge forward or backwards if in reverse.

Moving Off and Stopping

Moving Off: Before moving off, follow the POSM routine: Prepare, Observe, Signal, and Move. Prepare your car, check mirrors and blind spots, signal right if necessary, and if it's clear and safe, move off. Stopping: To stop, use the MSM routine: Mirrors, Signal, Manoeuvre. Check internal mirror, left passenger mirror, and left blind spot. Signal left if needed, then carry out the stopping manoeuvre slowly, looking around and maintaining control. Speed can increase as confidence and ability improve.

Mirrors

Mirrors are indispensable tools for safe driving, providing a vital window into the surrounding environment. During a driving test, the examiner expects a seamless integration of mirror usage into the driver's routine, emphasising continuous monitoring over occasional checks during specific manoeuvres. Each instance of incorrect mirror usage typically results in a minor fault. Learning to incorporate the use of mirrors into the mirror-signal-manoeuvre routine, covering blind spots, and maintaining awareness of other road users is essential. Quick, efficient glances should be the norm, avoiding prolonged distractions from the road ahead. Mirror checks become increasingly crucial with higher speeds, influencing speed adjustments, lane changes, overtaking manoeuvres, and responses to potential hazards. Checking mirrors before pulling away, changing lanes, overtaking, turning, slowing down, stopping, and opening doors forms a comprehensive approach to mirror use, ensuring drivers are well-prepared to navigate various driving scenarios safely and confidently

Blind spot

A blind spot refers to the area not covered by mirrors. Relying solely on mirrors, cameras, or detectors without personal visual assessment may lead to accidents. To check the blind spot, turn your head and look over your shoulder, ensuring safety before manoeuvring. Blind spot checks are essential when moving off, stopping, turning into a side road, navigating junctions and roundabouts, and merging or changing lanes on motorways. Awareness of being in another driver's blind spot is crucial, especially around larger vehicles like HGVs. Maintaining a safe distance and observing their indicators can prevent potential accidents. Why not practice turning your head while stationary to integrate this into your regular observation routine for safe driving.

Speed Limit

Speed limit signs play a crucial role in regulating vehicle speeds, and is a key part of the Highway Code. Residential roads commonly have a 30 mph speed limit, marked by repeater signs in areas without street lighting. Repeater signs also indicate speed limits on roads with different speed constraints. Vigilance is essential, as not all roads may have repeater signs. For single carriageways, the national speed limit for cars and motorcycles is typically 60 mph while on dual carriageways it is 70 mph. These limits are determined by the presence of a central reservation, not the number of lanes. It's important to recognise that speed limits are not targets; drivers can adjust their speed up to the limit if conditions permit. Drivers bear the responsibility of knowing the speed limit for the road they are on, and signs are typically located near turns, junctions, roundabouts, country road entries and exits, and transitions from residential areas to faster road layouts. Awareness of speed limit changes is crucial for safe and lawful driving.

Junctions

Junctions are meeting points of two or more roads, crucial parts of the road system. In the UK, they come in diverse shapes and sizes, and it's vital to navigate them safely as they are accident-prone areas. According to the Highway Code, you should be cautious of pedestrians, cyclists, and motorcyclists, give-way to those crossing, and remain behind certain vehicles. Different junction types include T-junctions, open junctions with good visibility, closed junctions requiring reduced speed, marked junctions with road signs, unmarked junctions in rural areas, controlled junctions with traffic lights, yellow box junctions easing traffic flow, staggered junctions with two or more minor roads, and roundabouts where traffic circulates around a central island. Always give way to the right at roundabouts, and use signals appropriately.

Traffic Lights

Traffic lights convey safety instructions to road users in the UK. The sequence includes stopping at red, preparing to go at red and amber, proceeding when the way is clear at green, and stopping (unless unsafe) at amber. Stop lines at traffic lights are mandatory, and some may have separate sections for cyclists. Filter traffic lights with arrows indicate priority directions, and during outages, approach with caution and treat the situation like an unmarked junction where no one has priority.

Roundabouts

Roundabouts in the UK are junctions where traffic circulates clockwise, prioritising those already on and those approaching from the right. Indicated by a triangular sign, they aim to maintain traffic flow without traffic lights. Mini roundabouts, identified by a blue sign with clockwise arrows, may lack road markings but are preceded by a sign. Traffic light-controlled roundabouts enhance safety, eliminating the need to yield to the right. Still, check for any unexpected movements, especially emergency vehicles. For left turns (1st exit), use the left lane, keep the indicator on, and exit via the first turn. Straight-ahead (usually 2nd exit) may use the left lane, but larger roundabouts might have specific lanes or markings. Signal left after the first exit and exit if clear. Right turns (typically 3rd exit) require the right-hand lane, moving to the left before exiting, signalled after passing the 2nd exit. Check mirrors and blind spots for a smooth transition.

Eco-Driving

Eco-driving focuses on minimising fuel usage and environmental impact while driving. The advantages include reduced fuel costs, lower exhaust emissions (including CO2), less vehicle wear, and improved safety. Techniques involve avoiding abrupt braking, moderating acceleration, using higher gears for lower engine revs, maintaining a steady speed, minimising excess weight, planning efficient routes, avoiding unnecessary idling, and keeping tires properly inflated. Considering an eco-friendly vehicle is also recommended for a comprehensive approach to environmentally conscious driving.

Satellite Navigation / Road Signs

Satellite navigation can be used through a car's integrated system, a detachable device, or a mobile Sat-Nav app. Inputting a postcode allows it to calculate a route, offering options like the fastest, shortest, or most economical. Voice guidance and on-screen directions assist drivers. When using a mobile Sat-Nav app, ensure the phone is securely mounted. Various road markings include stop lines at signals, 'give way' lines, edge lines, centre lines, hazard warning lines, and double white lines with specific rules for each. Lane lines and markings along the road's edge relate to parking and waiting rules. 'Keep clear' sections are labelled to indicate no-stopping areas, and labelled lanes help drivers navigate complex junctions on wider roads and motorways.

Adverse Weather Driving

Driving in challenging weather demands extra caution and preparation. If possible, delay non-essential trips during adverse conditions. When facing rain, ice, or snow, maintain a safe distance, keep headlights on, slow down, and use higher gears to prevent skidding. In hail, ensure good visibility, reduce speed, and find a safe spot during severe conditions. For foggy conditions, drive sensibly, maintain distance, and use fog lights if visibility drops below 100 meters. In winter, stay alert, accelerate and decelerate gradually, slow down before turns, avoid sudden stops, and refrain from using cruise control. These measures enhance safety and help navigate various weather challenges effectively.
